Nash Push / Fold

When stacks get short, the tree collapses to two actions: shove or fold. Here are the charts.

Why push/fold becomes the solved subgame

Under ~12bb, opening to 2.2bb and folding to a shove is a disaster — you lose ~20% of your stack without a showdown. The solver solution collapses to open-shove or fold: a simple, 2-player zero-sum push/fold game that John Nash's equilibrium concept solves exactly. "Nash ranges" are the closed-form solution.

How ICM bends Nash

The charts above are pure chip-EV (no ICM). At the bubble or final table, ICM pressure compresses both ranges — more so on the calling side.

  • Shover tightens ~20%. The downside of losing a shove is steeper; marginal shoves with 85o become folds.
  • Caller tightens 30-50%. Losing a flip busts; even AQ becomes a fold vs. a covering shove at extreme bubbles.

The result: under ICM pressure, the big stack actually gets to shove wider than Nash, because the short/medium stacks can't profitably call. This is the origin of "big stack bullying."

Re-shove (ISO shove) ranges

When someone opens to 2.2bb ahead of you and you have 10-18bb, re-shoving is often better than flatting. You get dead money (the raise and the blinds) with fold equity against the opener's wide range.

A rough re-shove range facing a BTN 2.2bb open with 15bb from the SB:

  • Pure value shoves: 88+, AJ+
  • Thin value shoves: 55-77, AT, KQ, some AQ-AJ suited
  • Bluff shoves: small suited Ax (A2s-A5s), some suited broadways (KJs, QJs) as mixes
  • Total: ~18-22% of hands
Shortcut for live play: if you can't remember a chart, use this rule — at 10bb, open-shove any pair, any suited ace, any broadway, and any suited connector 76s+ from BTN or later. Tighten by one tier per position you move earlier.
Don't memorize Nash in isolation. Nash assumes chip-EV and a single-table context. At real tables with ICM, multiple players behind you, and pay jumps, the ranges shift. Use Nash as a ceiling, tighten from there.
