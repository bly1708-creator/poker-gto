Bubble & Final Table

Pay jumps distort chip equity in specific, predictable ways. Learn the patterns and exploit them.

The money bubble

The money bubble is the single biggest ICM inflection point. One player gets zero; the next gets min-cash. The equity gap between 2nd-last and last is effectively infinite (min-cash vs. $0).

Effect: everyone except the chip leader plays tight to ladder. The chip leader gets to open every button, 3-bet from the SB, and 4-bet-bluff short stacks who can't call without tanking their equity.

Bubble strategy by stack

  • Chip leader: open 50%+ from BTN, 40%+ from SB. Iso-shove over min-opens from medium stacks.
  • Big stack (not leader): play chip-EV-ish preflop, tighten calls vs. chip leader's aggression.
  • Medium stack (most risk): open tight, fold medium pairs to covering 3-bets, never flip if you can avoid it.
  • Short stack: counterintuitively, open-shove wider than Nash — you get folds from the mediums who are ICM-scared.
  • Shortest stack (the bubble stack): fold everything except premiums; the next blind might bust someone else and you cash.

The pay-jump ladder

Every pay jump is its own mini-bubble. Final-table ladders typically look like:

Finish% of prize pool% gain over next-lowest
1st22%+47%
2nd15%+36%
3rd11%+38%
4th8%+33%
5th6%+50%
6th4%+33%
7th3%+50%
8th2%+100%
9th1%baseline

Notice how the bottom jumps (9th → 8th) are smaller in absolute dollars but huge in percentage. A short stack has far more to protect than a big stack does.

Covering stacks weaponize ICM

If you cover another player, you can bust them. That threat creates leverage even in spots where chip-EV would say call:

  • Chip leader opens SB. You're BB with JJ and 30bb. Chip leader covers.
  • Chip-EV: 3-bet shove for value. JJ is a monster.
  • ICM: flat. Shoving risks your whole stack for a flip vs. AK-AQ and worse than coinflip vs. QQ+. The $EV of just calling and playing a postflop pot is higher.

Final table stack structure

A typical 9-handed final table has one big stack (40-60bb), a couple mid stacks (20-30bb), and multiple short stacks (7-15bb). The big stack's job is to pressure the mids while shorts do their own push/fold thing.

Playing big stack at the FT

  • Open 45%+ from late position.
  • Iso-shove over min-opens from mids when you cover.
  • Don't tangle with other big stacks — they can hurt you.
  • Limp SB vs. short BB to keep them in bad spots; they can't fold to your option raise cheaply.

Playing mid stack at the FT

  • This is the hardest stack to navigate.
  • Tighten preflop calls — especially vs. the big stack.
  • Look for spots to shove over shorts who are limping or min-opening.
  • Avoid flipping against covering stacks at all costs.

Playing short stack at the FT

  • Your job is to double up or bust trying. Ladder income comes from others busting, not from you surviving.
  • Open-shove wider than mid stacks — against ICM-scared mediums you get folds you don't deserve.
  • Don't flat opens unless it's a pot you can commit to.

Specific bubble spots

The "I'm covered by BB" open

You have 15bb on the button. BB has 40bb and covers. Your default open range tightens sharply — BB will 3-bet shove wide knowing you can't call. Fold 72s, J8o, K3o — hands you'd open normally — and open only combos you'd be happy to call a shove with (22+, A9+, KT+, suited broadway).

The "I cover the shortest BB" open

You have 30bb, BTN. BB has 5bb. Open nearly any two — BB can't call wide without risking their cash, and their re-shove range is narrow enough that you just fold your steals to it.

Memory aid: the three stacks to respect at a FT are "covers me," "I cover for cash," and "two big jumps from now." Everyone else is ordinary chip-EV.
