ICM Fundamentals

In tournaments, chips are not dollars. This single fact rewrites every range you learned for cash.

The core asymmetry

In a cash game, every chip is worth its face value. Win 100 chips, make $1. Lose 100 chips, lose $1. Symmetric.

In a tournament, chips have diminishing value. The first chip you stack from 50bb to 51bb is worth more than the 100th chip you stack from 149bb to 150bb. You can't cash out chips mid-tournament; they're just a score that decides your finishing position, which then maps to prize money.

The Independent Chip Model (ICM)

ICM converts chip stacks to expected dollar value by calculating each player's probability of finishing in each paying position.

The simplest version (Malmuth-Harville) assumes each player's probability of winning is proportional to their chip stack. So if you have 40% of all chips in play, you win the tournament 40% of the time in the model. Second place probabilities are then computed recursively: given that player X wins, what's each remaining player's chance of finishing second?

Worked example: 3-way with prize pool ($500 / $300 / $200)

Stacks: A = 60k, B = 30k, C = 10k. Total = 100k.

PlayerStack %P(1st)P(2nd)P(3rd)$EV
A60%60%30%10%$410
B30%30%48%22%$338
C10%10%22%68%$252

Notice: A has 60% of chips but only 41% of the equity ($410 / $1000). That 20% gap is the ICM tax paid by the chip leader.

Risk premium

Because chip EV and $EV diverge, you can't just play "chip-EV-neutral" in a tournament. Risk premium is the extra equity you need to break even in $ terms on a risky decision.

Risk premium = required equity ($EV breakeven) – required equity (chip-EV breakeven)

Example: calling an all-in needs 50% equity in chip EV. Under ICM, the same decision might need 57% equity because losing the flip costs you $30 but winning only gains $20. That 7% gap is the risk premium.

The four rules of ICM

  1. Survival is worth money. Big stacks can afford variance; short stacks cannot.
  2. Covering stacks apply pressure. If you can eliminate me, you have leverage — even if we're both deep.
  3. Medium stacks suffer. Between short stacks that ladder and covering stacks that apply pressure, medium stacks have the worst hand of any stack.
  4. ICM dissolves in heads-up. Prize jumps are spent; it's winner-take-remainder chip-EV.

Chip EV vs $EV: a concrete flip

Bubble of an MTT. You have 25bb, covering an opponent's 20bb shove. You have AK. Against villain's shoving range, you have 58% equity.

  • Chip-EV call: trivially +EV. Call snap.
  • ICM call: requires 63% equity. At 58%, this is a fold.

The difference: losing 20bb puts you on 5bb (essentially dead). Winning 20bb puts you on 45bb (still plenty). The downside is steeper than the upside.

Study tool: ICMizer, HoldemResources Calculator (HRC), and DTO are the standard ICM trainers. They let you plug in stack sizes and payouts, then see the exact EV of each action. Every serious MTT player runs hands through one of these regularly.
