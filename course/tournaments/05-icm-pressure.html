Applying ICM Pressure

The capstone — risk premium, Future Game Simulation, and the math of deal-making.

Risk premium as a number

We introduced risk premium conceptually in Lesson 1. Now let's quantify it. Risk premium (RP) is the extra equity required for a $EV-breakeven call above the chip-EV breakeven:

RP = required %$EV equity – required %chipEV equity

Risk premium scales with two factors:

  • Pay jump steepness. Bigger jumps = higher RP.
  • Stack asymmetry. The more villain covers you, the higher RP on your calls.

Reference RP values at a final table

ScenarioRP
Chip leader calling 20bb shove from short~2%
Mid stack calling 15bb shove from similar stack~5%
Mid stack calling 20bb shove from covering big stack~10%
Short stack calling 10bb shove from covering mid stack~7%
Covered stack on 3-handed bubble calling a shove~15-20%

FGS — Future Game Simulation

ICM assumes the tournament ends right now in the proportions indicated by current stacks. FGS is an extension that models N additional future hands with positional and skill edges baked in. It's more accurate but slower and more computationally expensive.

Practically, FGS produces slightly different equilibria at points where skill edge matters — a skilled player is worth "more" than their chip stack suggests, because they'll outperform ICM's lottery assumption in future hands. The main implication: if you're a better-than-average player at the final table, you should be marginally more willing to survive than raw ICM suggests.

Applying pressure: the "unexploitable bully"

Big stacks at a bubble have an unexploitable advantage. Their shoving and 3-betting ranges widen more than the calling ranges of mediums can tighten. Even if the mediums know exactly what the big stack is doing, they still can't correctly call — the RP is too high.

Concrete bubble shove ranges for a 60bb chip leader against covered mids:

  • Open raise BTN: 60%+ (adds K5o, Q8o, J7o, T7o, 84s).
  • Open shove BTN with 25bb effective behind: 35%+ when mediums are very ICM-bound.
  • SB vs covered BB: nearly any two — 80%+.
  • 3-bet vs medium opens: shove 15-20% IP.
Counter-play: if you're the medium stack being bullied, the only way to stop the bleed is to pick one spot and go. Find a clean 3-bet shove range (22+, AJ+, KQs type) and deploy it hours in. The bully has to respect the re-shove; their own ICM math gets messy if their open range is 60% and your re-shove range is now credible.

Deal-making: the ICM chop

When a final table reaches deal-making territory, ICM provides the baseline chop: each player's $EV according to the Malmuth-Harville model at current stacks. Variants:

  • Full ICM chop: each player gets exactly their ICM $EV. Standard and mathematically fair.
  • Chip chop: each player gets prize money in proportion to chip stack. Favors the chip leader.
  • ICM + save for 1st: chop the minimum ICM amounts, leave a portion for the winner. Satisfies players who want to play for something.

Negotiating ICM deals

If you're the chip leader, push for chip chop — you get more than your ICM equity. If you're short, push for ICM (or better-than-ICM if the leader is deal-averse). Mid stacks usually benefit most from ICM + leave-for-1st structures.

Standard leverage tools:

  • Refuse unfavorable chops; time is your friend if opponents are more deal-hungry than you.
  • Offer a "compromise" between ICM and chip chop — 50/50 split is a common middle ground.
  • Compute the exact ICM numbers before the conversation starts. Never accept a chop without running the math.

Wrap-up: the MTT player's mental model

Every tournament decision maps to three questions:

  1. What stage am I in? Early, mid, bubble, FT, HU.
  2. What is the effective stack? That determines the tree.
  3. What's the risk premium on this decision? Compare to chip-EV baseline; adjust range accordingly.

Master those three, and every range you memorize becomes a living thing that adjusts to the table in front of you — which is exactly what a solver does.

Final reminder: ICM is a tool. It's not a different game. The underlying GTO framework — ranges, frequencies, blockers, polarization — is the same. ICM just modifies the payoff matrix. Once you see it that way, tournament study and cash study reinforce each other instead of feeling separate.
