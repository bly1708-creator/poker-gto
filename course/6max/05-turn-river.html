Turn and River Play

Barreling, overbets, and how to construct a river range that villain's bluff-catchers can't beat.

The turn: where ranges get refined

Roughly 50% of your flop range folds to the turn barrel. That means every turn decision prunes your range. A GTO turn plan has three components:

  1. Continuing range: which combos stay in your betting line?
  2. Sizing: small (blocker/denial) or big (polar)?
  3. Give-up candidates: which flop bluffs with no equity don't double-barrel?

Turn barrel criteria (in order of importance)

  • Turn card changes equity? Overcards and flush/straight completers tighten villain's range.
  • Turn card helps you more than villain? Compare how many combos each side gains.
  • Blocker quality? Turn cards that pair villain's range (K-high texture turns a K) drop barrel frequency, since villain now has more calls.
  • Equity when called? A naked gutshot is a better bluff than a king-high with no draw.

Overbetting

An overbet (125-200% pot) appears in solver solutions whenever one side has a massive nut advantage and villain has too many medium-strength hands in his range. The conditions:

  • You have sets, straights, flushes. Villain has only top pair.
  • The pot is deep enough for the overbet to still threaten stacks.
  • The board is static — no more key cards coming.

Classic overbet spots:

  • BTN vs BB, 9s6h2d-Js turn: BTN has all the JJ/JT/J9 combos. Overbet pot.
  • UTG vs BB 3-bet pot, K72r-K brick turn: UTG has all AA/KK/AK. Overbet for thin value.
  • Any paired river after SRP checked down flop + turn bet by PFR: PFR polarizes the river with overbet shoves.
Overbet requires a bluff mix. If you overbet 125% pot, you need ~1 bluff per 1.1 value combos. That's a lot of bluffs. If you don't have credible bluff candidates, overbetting only for value is exploitable.

River: the polarization endgame

On the river, every hand is either value, bluff, or give-up. No more equity realization, no more draws. Your range collapses to three categories.

Picking river bluffs: blockers dominate

On the river, bluff selection is almost entirely about blockers, because every candidate has 0% equity when called. The best bluffs:

  • Block villain's strongest calls. On a flush-completed river, bluff with the Ax of the flush suit — blocks nut flushes.
  • Unblock his folds. If villain's folding range is missed draws, hold a card that unblocks those missed draws.
  • Low showdown value. Don't turn a bluff-catcher (ace-high) into a bluff — that hand is more valuable as a check-call.

Calling rivers: bluff-catcher selection

You don't call with your best hand, and you don't call with your worst. You call with the correct amount of hands — the ones that beat bluffs. The key insight: all bluff-catchers have the same EV at equilibrium.

So pick the combos that block villain's value. If the board is A-K-7-4-3 and you have K9 vs AJ, holding a K reduces villain's two-pair combos. That's a better call than 99, even though 99 looks "stronger" — both lose to all value and beat all bluffs, so you want the one that removes value combos.

Triple-barrel construction

A clean way to build your triple-barrel range, given flop + turn + river:

  1. Flop: start with your opening range × flop c-bet frequency.
  2. Turn: continue ~50% of that range. Prune give-ups (flop bluffs with no turn equity).
  3. River: continue ~40% of your turn range. Final polarization: value combos that beat bluff-catchers + blocker bluffs.

Multiplying through: if you c-bet 70% of your opening range, double-barrel 50% of that, triple-barrel 40% of that — your river bet is 14% of your original opening range. That's a narrow, strong range. Good.

Common leak: triple-barreling too often. If you bet flop, turn, and river with every hand that started as a c-bet, your river range is too wide and too bluff-heavy. Villain calls light and you lose.
